# Adversary review (round 2) — ADHD anti-pattern skeptic

Lens: will this dashboard overwhelm, nag, guilt, or quietly become another shiny thing that gets avoided? Where does it violate calm-by-default, one-thing, or shame-free? Authority for every claim is the recon (E-lanes + round2 R-docs) and Mark's own ADHD profile, the body of work the plan is supposed to honor. Round 1 already flagged the missing carrots (done-lane, ritual, re-roll, idle floor, capped hero). The round-2 plan absorbed all seven round-1 defects (D1->1.5/M8b, D2->5.2, D3->1.9/M14, D4->1.6, D5->1.4, D6->4.3, D7->8.3). Good. So this pass attacks the NEW failure surface those fixes created, plus the high-confidence recon findings the round-2 plan still drops or under-specs.

Verdict: the round-2 plan over-corrected. Round 1 said "all stick, no carrot." Round 2 bolted on five separate interactive ADHD mechanisms into Phase 1 (re-roll, lock-in, stall-interrupt-into-Focus-mode, capture, done-lane settle) and now the hero card is a control panel, not one thing. The plan that opens by quoting "surface ONE recommended next thing... turn the volume down on everything else" then hangs four controls on that one thing. That is the contradiction at the center of this revision. The mechanical calm layer is still excellent; the new motivational layer needs pruning before it becomes its own wall.

---

## D1 (HIGH) — The hero card now carries four controls; "one thing, one action" became a control panel

Where: Section 1.1 ("The hero carries one primary action button. Copy and the other action demote to small quiet icon buttons"), then Section 6.3 piles on: "One primary `Button`... Secondary actions as small quiet icon buttons. A quiet 'Not now, show me another' re-roll control (1.6) and a 'Lock this in as today's one thing' control (1.9)." Plus the saturation-capped age band (1.4) and, under stall, the Focus-mode emphasis (1.8).

Why it fails the lens: count the interactive affordances the round-2 hero renders at rest: primary action button, 2+ secondary icon buttons (copy, powershell), the re-roll, the lock-in. That is five decisions on the single card whose entire job (E1 P2, E4 P8, E5 anti-pattern 7, user profile "1 recommendation not 10 options") is to kill the choice before it paralyzes. E1 AP4 names this exactly: "Five same-size buttons per item... forces a micro-decision per item." The plan's own Section 1.1 says the secondary actions "demote to small quiet icon buttons," but adding re-roll + lock-in on top means the hero now asks: do this, or copy it, or open a shell, or skip it, or commit to it for the day. For an oscillating ADHD brain, "should I lock this in or re-roll it?" is a fresh decision loop layered on top of the task decision the card was supposed to remove. The re-roll (1.6) and the lock-in (1.9) are semantic opposites (defer vs commit) sitting side by side; presenting both at once is the menu-of-equal-options trap (E7 C7) wearing a calm coat.

Round 1 asked for the re-roll AND the lock-in as separate fixes. Round 2 added both. Neither round noticed that putting both on the hero simultaneously re-creates the choice overload each was individually meant to relieve.

Minimal fix: the hero shows at most ONE deferral affordance and ONE commitment affordance, and never both prominently at the same time. Concretely: the primary action is the only button at full weight. Re-roll is a single small "not now" affordance. The lock-in is NOT a always-present second control; it appears only in the morning-intake/first-open moment (which is where E7 F1/F3 actually put it: "lock it in?" is the intake gesture, not a persistent hero button), and after lock-in the hero shows the commitment-mirror state with re-roll still reachable but no second "lock" button (it is already locked). That collapses the at-rest hero back to: one primary action, one quiet "not now," one quiet copy. State this affordance budget in 6.3 as a hard cap, the same way Section 1.1 caps dominance levels at three.

## D2 (HIGH) — Three involuntary/voluntary interrupts stack on the same surface with no arbitration; the stall-interrupt can fire mid-decision

Where: Section 1.6 (manual re-roll, voluntary), 1.8 (stall pattern-interrupt collapses to Focus mode after ~25s of no action, involuntary, default ON), 1.5/M8b (the `justResolved` settle beat, 150-200ms), plus the data re-read updating the list in place every ~20s.

Why it fails the lens: the plan adds a 25s stall timer that, on no interaction, collapses the whole Home to Focus mode "with a single `motion-safe:` emphasis on the primary button" (1.8). Consider the actual ADHD moment this targets: Mark is staring at the hero, oscillating, NOT acting, which is precisely decision paralysis. The plan's response is to mutate the screen out from under him (collapse to Focus, emphasize the button) at the 25s mark. That is uninvited motion fired at the exact instant the user is most overwhelmed, and E4 P6/A5 (no uninvited interruptions, "unexpected interactions disrupt focus and create sensory overload") and E4 A6 (no auto-relayout that moves the eye) both forbid a layout change the user did not request. The plan tries to thread this with "any pointer or key activity cancels it" and "never blocks input," but a user frozen in decision paralysis is BY DEFINITION not moving the pointer, so the collapse fires on exactly the population it claims to exempt. "The interface is the interrupt" (E1 P8) is real, but E1 frames it as "collapse to just the one card, or pulse the primary button" as ALTERNATIVES, and the plan chose to do the collapse (a relayout) rather than the gentler in-place pulse.

Compounding: there is no arbitration between the three motion sources. A `justResolved` settle (1.5) can fire while the stall timer is counting; the ~20s poll can reorder the sub-dominant list while the user reads it; the 25s collapse can land right after a poll re-read shifts the rows. The plan never says which wins or that they are mutually exclusive. Three independent things animating the calm surface is the "page never sits still" anti-pattern (E4 A6, the plan's own 1.2 "no uninvited motion") that round 1 praised the plan for avoiding, now reintroduced through the carrots.

Minimal fix: (1) make the stall response in-place, not a relayout: pulse/emphasize the primary button and dim the periphery via opacity, no grid collapse, so nothing moves position (honor 6.1's "hero never reflows"). If a true Focus collapse is wanted, gate it behind an explicit user tap, never an automatic timer. (2) Default the stall-interrupt OFF, not ON (the plan's open question 6 already doubts this; resolve it toward OFF). An automatic screen mutation is the highest-risk ADHD affordance in the plan and should be opt-in, per E4 F10 / E3 P5 (ambient, never seize attention). (3) Add one sentence: only one motion source runs at a time; a pending settle defers the stall timer, and a poll re-read never animates row movement (keyed-diff content updates only, which 1.2 already promises). Make that a test in M13.

## D3 (HIGH) — Phase 1 ships five new ADHD mechanisms at once; this is the "fusion" anti-pattern the plan warns about, applied to features instead of repos

Where: Section 7 MVP line. Phase 1b crams M12 (capture + remote channel), M13 (stall-interrupt), M14 (lock-in), plus M8b (done-lane settle) and M9-strip-floor, "each gated on real use" but all inside Phase 1 before ship.

Why it fails the lens: the plan's own discipline (Section 8 intro: "ship the dumb capture+list+done store first... then add horizons, categories, parking... so Phase 2 does not repeat a fusion") is the right instinct, and Phase 1 violates it. Five behavioral mechanisms (capture, done-payoff, re-roll, lock-in, stall-collapse) land together with no sequencing for which one earns its place. For an ADHD user, an app that arrives with five new interaction patterns to learn is itself overwhelm on first run (E4 P3 progressive disclosure, E5 P6 "do NOT churn the whole layout"), and the round-1 review's core warning ("the shiny thing that gets avoided") applies doubly to a feature-dense v1 that hasn't proven any single mechanism works for Mark. The plan says each carrot is "gated on real use," but they are all in the same pre-ship phase, so none of them actually gets a real-use gate before the next lands. "Gated on real use" with five features shipping together is a label, not a gate.

This is the adversary's strongest structural objection: the recon's whole thesis is restraint (one thing, calm, don't manufacture noise), and the round-2 response to "you were too thin" was to get thick. The correct response to "missing the payoff loop" is the done-lane (1.5), full stop. Re-roll, lock-in, and stall-interrupt are each defensible but are three more bets, and bundling them hides which bet failed when Mark stops opening the app.

Minimal fix: re-cut the Phase 1 MVP to the load-bearing carrot only. Phase 1a (already lean) plus M8b (done-lane payoff) plus M12 (capture, because object permanence is genuinely load-bearing, E1 4.7/AP5). Defer M13 (stall-interrupt) and M14 (lock-in) to a "Phase 1c" that ships only after Mark has used the lean version and reports the hero either locks onto a dreaded item (then ship lock-in/re-roll) or that he oscillates on Home (then ship stall-interrupt). The re-roll (1.6) is cheap and can stay in 1b since it is the pressure valve for the deterministic ranker. Make the phase boundary a real decision point with Mark, not a milestone list. This costs nothing but ordering and is the plan honoring its own anti-fusion rule.

## D4 (MEDIUM) — The avoidance-category pin, the one mechanism that fights Mark's actual documented failure mode, is entirely Phase 2

Where: Section 1.4 ("The avoidance categories (Phase 2)..."), Section 4.4, Section 8.4 (the keyword classifier), Section 5.3 Tier 5 (Phase 2).

Why it fails the lens: the entire reason this app exists, per the user profile and E7's load-bearing observation, is the six avoidance areas that "carry no intrinsic interest and no natural deadline, so they rot quietly" (E7 §1, E5 §1, E1 P5). E1 P5 / E3 §1 are explicit: avoidance items are "not-now" and therefore invisible, and the dashboard's job is to "drag the right not-now item into now." The round-2 plan's Phase 1 ranking is driven entirely by git recency and the producer's needs-you (Tier 4: hotter ageColor, then `gitAgeDays` ascending). But avoidance items have NO git activity by definition (E7 7, Section 8.4 says this outright: "items with no commits have no recency to age them, so without the pin the exact avoidance items most likely to rot are the ones the board cannot escalate"). So Phase 1 systematically cannot surface the exact items the app was built to surface. Phase 1 is a program-status board ranked by code activity, which is a fine thing, but it is program-board's job, and the workspace CLAUDE.md already runs program-board for that. The differentiated value (avoidance accountability) is 100% deferred.

This is not asking to build the Phase 2 todo store early. The classifier (8.4) is described as a "renderer-side PURE keyword classifier" over the program-board `blocked_on`/`needs_you` text the Phase 1 feed already carries. That is Phase-1-feasible against the existing feed. Mark's avoidance areas (financial confrontation, documentation, delegation, completing-the-loop, health, marketing) will appear in `blocked_on`/needs-you strings on real program cards today.

Minimal fix: pull the keyword classifier (8.4) forward to Phase 1 as a read-only PIN signal on program cards only (no todo store needed): an item whose `blocked_on`/needs-you text matches an avoidance keyword stays in the needs-you band even when its git recency would let it sink. It does not need to write, log, or feed `composeClaudeQuery` (8.4 already forbids those). This is one pure function + one Tier-4 tie-break adjustment, gated by a test, and it is the difference between "another status board" and "the thing that catches the financial follow-up before it rots." If it must stay Phase 2, then Section 1 and Risk R-8 must say plainly that Phase 1 cannot surface no-git-activity avoidance items, so the gap is a stated decision, not a silent one, exactly the standard round 1 set for the ritual gap (and which the plan adopted at 1.9).

## D5 (MEDIUM) — The "N closed today" payoff resets at midnight and can read as zero right when Mark needs the win

Where: Section 1.5 ("a quiet 'N closed today' line"), Section 4.3 caught-up state pairs "Clear. Keep working." with "N closed today" (1.5), Section 6.3 header.

Why it partially fails the lens: the done-lane is the round-1 carrot and the plan implemented it (good). But the payoff is sourced from "program cards crossing into the Done lane plus `requires_response` sessions that have been answered" within the current calendar day (1.5). Two failure modes for an ADHD brain: (1) Early morning, the count is 0. The first thing Mark sees on the caught-up state is "Clear. Keep working." paired with "0 closed today," which reads as "you've done nothing," the opposite of the momentum beat E4 F9 / E5 P2 demand. A payoff that displays zero is worse than no payoff; it is a quiet guilt number. (2) The count is derived from program-board cards crossing lanes, which depends on git commits/DoD changes Mark may make in bursts or not at all on a given day (he is away from the terminal most of the day per Lane G). So the "win" metric is driven by code-commit cadence, not by Mark actually closing loops, and on a meeting-heavy day it under-counts real accomplishment and shows a demotivating low number.

Minimal fix: (1) Suppress the count entirely when it is 0, rather than rendering "0 closed today." A payoff that has nothing to pay shows nothing, never a zero. (E5 anti-pattern: never make the undone/empty the focus.) (2) Consider a rolling window ("closed recently" / last 24h) rather than a hard calendar-midnight reset so an early-morning glance still reflects yesterday evening's wins; or scope the claim honestly to "closed today" only when >0. (3) State in 1.5 that the metric is acknowledged-best-effort (it tracks board-visible closes, not all of Mark's work), so it is never framed as a complete accomplishment ledger he can feel measured against. One conditional render + one comment; no architecture.

## D6 (MEDIUM) — The count-up 1s tick on the active tab is the one motion exception, and it runs forever with no calm ceiling

Where: Section 1.2 ("the single active-tab elapsed counter which is allowed a 1s tick because count-up on active work is itself the anti-time-blindness feature (E3 P7)"), Section 6.4 (the active-tab 1s count-up).

Why it partially fails the lens: granting count-up over countdown is correct (E3 P7). But the plan permits a perpetual 1-second-ticking number on the always-on Home surface with no upper bound or decay. E3 P7's intent is "you've been here a while" awareness, not a stopwatch. A digit changing every second in the periphery is exactly the "animated/looping motion in the periphery" E4 A7 bans ("a spinner per session is peripheral motion overload"), and for a time-blind user a number racing upward on a stalled-feeling task can manufacture the time-anxiety E3 P7 explicitly says count-up exists to AVOID. The plan correctly killed the per-session spinner under reduced-motion (1.2, good catch on `animate-spin`), then re-added a per-second-updating element that is arguably more attention-grabbing than a spin.

Minimal fix: tick the active count-up at a calm cadence (the ~20s poll cadence, or coarsen to minute resolution after the first minute: "1m", "2m", not "1:03, 1:04, 1:05"). The anti-time-blindness signal is "running ~40 min," which needs minute granularity, not second granularity, on an ambient surface. Reserve true 1s ticking, if ever, for an explicit focus/body-double timer the user started (E7 F7), not the ambient Home. Gate the cadence behind `prefers-reduced-motion` too (coarsen to the poll tick). One interval-rate change.

## D7 (LOW) — "Lock this in as today's one thing" with a deterministic ranker can strand Mark on a thing he can't finish, and the pin only clears on resolve/re-roll/new-day

Where: Section 1.9 (the lock pins above auto-rank "until it resolves or is re-rolled"), Section 5 (pinned-hero-id applied on top of the deterministic order), M14.

Why it's a latent failure: lock-in is the commitment mirror (E7 F2, good). But the failure path: Mark locks in a hard avoidance item in the morning (the intended use), works other things all day, never finishes it, and every glance at Home for the rest of the day shows the un-finished commitment pinned as the hero. E7 P4/C2 and E5 P7 are explicit that escalating salience on an avoided item must never become shame; a self-declared commitment you can SEE you failed to keep, pinned and staring back at you every visit until midnight, is a more personal guilt object than an OVERDUE stamp, because Mark put it there himself. The plan caps the hero's age-color (1.4, good) but does not consider that the lock-in mechanism itself manufactures a self-shame loop on exactly the long-horizon items most likely to not-finish-in-a-day. The only exits are resolve (may not happen), re-roll (the plan says re-roll clears the pin, M14, so the escape exists, good), or new-day.

Minimal fix: the re-roll-clears-the-pin behavior (M14) is the right valve; make sure 1.9/6.3 surface the re-roll WITH the locked commitment-mirror state (the plan says "re-roll still reachable" in Focus mode but should also be reachable on the normal locked hero). Add: a locked item that has been pinned but un-acted for the bulk of the day softens its framing at the evening cue toward "carry to tomorrow?" (the E7 F6 close-the-day move), never "you didn't do your one thing." That reframes an unmet commitment as a hand-off, not a failure. One copy/state addition, tied to the cue-binding the plan already plans for Phase 2; if the evening cue is Phase 2, then for Phase 1 simply ensure the locked hero never adds any "still not done" / time-elapsed-since-lock language. State that in 6.6's copy rules.

## What round 2 got right (so the fixes don't regress it)

- Absorbed all seven round-1 defects with real specs and milestones (done-lane 1.5/M8b, idle floor 5.2, ritual 1.9/M14, re-roll 1.6, capped hero 1.4, caught-up payoff 4.3, triage constraints 8.3/8.8). The plan listens.
- The idle-age floor (5.2) is implemented correctly and tested (M6/M9), and it fixes the badge-soup firehose round 1 flagged; "N need you" now means something.
- Capped hero color, thin left-edge band, never a red fill (1.4): the right resolution of round 1 D5, and it cites the exact anti-patterns.
- Capture is genuinely one-gesture, sub-2s MEASURED not asserted (1.3, M12), zero required fields: textbook E1 4.7 / AP5.
- Shame-free copy discipline centralized with a test for em dashes and slop words (6.6): matches E5/E7/E1 guilt anti-patterns and the writing standard.
- Relative-time-only, count-up not countdown, one helper (1.2, M7): matches E3 P2/P7 (modulo the D6 cadence nit).
- `startupView` defaults to `lastSession`, Home opt-in (M11): correctly avoids the dead-splash double-gate (E4 F1, R-10).
- The triage-one-at-a-time + quiet-inbox-number constraints (8.3) are now fixed in the plan, not deferred to a stub: the round-1 D7 fix landed cleanly.

The round-2 risks above (D1, D2, D3 especially) are about pruning the new motivational layer back to calm-by-default and one-thing, not undoing the carrots. The plan swung from too thin to too busy. D1 (affordance cap on the hero), D2 (stall-interrupt in-place + default OFF), and D3 (don't ship five mechanisms at once) are the ones to resolve before Phase 1 ships; they are the difference between a calm board with a payoff and a control panel that re-creates the wall it was built to remove.
